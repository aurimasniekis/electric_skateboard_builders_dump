## \#52 Posted by: goldenHusky Posted at: 2017-04-01T23:11:27.774Z Reads: 472

```
[quote="Maxid, post:50, topic:15639"]
Doesn't the voltage have basically no effect on the heat? 1600W can be misleading since 1V 1600A versus 1600V 1A will generate completely different temperatures.
[/quote]

I think there won't be different temperatures, no matter if 1600V and 1A or 1600A and 1V. At least in the theory.

Basicially I¬≤R losses generate the heat, but also U*I since it's only ohmic load when using my resistor array for example. So if you have 1V and 1600A, you have U*I= 1600W dissipation power. To make it possible that 1600A flow at 1V the resistor needs to be 1/1600. So according to I¬≤R it's 1600¬≤*1/1600 which is also 1600.

Same when using 1600V and 1A. U*I is also 1600W in this case. You will need a resistor of 1600V/1A to allow only 1A to flow at 1600V, so again, according to I¬≤R it's 1¬≤*1600=1600W.

So voltage and current aren't really relevant if their product stays the same. I'm using a 5V/70A LED supply for testing my switches. I already did something like that with the DirectFet switches but with 55A if I remember correctly.

Edit:
I went for the 5V/70A for several reasons: A 40V/70A supply would be quite expensive and when testing the switch I only care about the heat generated on the switch itself and not on my resistors, so the heat is dependent on the resistance of the Fets and the traces. Of course you could also do it via the occuring voltage drop.. but who chooses the hard way? :joy:
```

## \#231 Posted by: goldenHusky Posted at: 2018-03-29T15:56:59.914Z Reads: 147

```
Hi @zblad
alright, worst case for the led is when your 12s battery pack is fully charged so the voltage will be  12*4.2V= 50.4V. 50.4 minus 12V (led requires 12V) is 38.4V and 38.4V divided through 20mA (the rated current of most led used in those switches) is 1920 ohms. So that is the minimum resistor value, however it's better to use a higher value resistor since most leds are bright enough at even lower currents like 10 mA.

Also keep an eye on the maximum dissipation power of the resistor you use.

[quote="zblad, post:230, topic:15639"]
the only one I can find is $70.00
[/quote]
It's probably a lot of 5000 pcs then, they also offer them in lower amounts but their website is kinda tricky.

[quote="zblad, post:230, topic:15639"]
I think I could figure it out if I knew the voltage drop of the LED but I don‚Äôt.
[/quote]
The description of your switch says 230VAC (that is the max. voltage at C, NC, NO) and 12V/15mA for the led rating.
```
