```
In cycling we have what are called weightweenies.
The name refers to people that care about the weight of their bikes.
Some try to lighten their bike without compromising functionality or durability while others are often going to extremes to shed few grams.
I belong to the first category but I am often impressed by the ingenuity of the second category.
Although I understand that power and range for electric skateboards cannot come without weight, I am curious if any of you have managed to build a light yet powerful board without making too many compromises.
So show us your ultralight builds.
```

```
It's a great question @taz - I used to run a bike shop and folks would put tremendous time and energy (and money) into shaving weight off of their bicycles. If you make every choice based on reducing weight, it adds up to a very lightweight ride.

My opinion - esk8 doesn't have enough advanced product offerings yet for people to dive in and think too much about weight - where are the titanium hardware kits and carbon fiber truck assemblies? Hollowed-out urethane wheels?
```

## \#26 Posted by: taz Posted at: 2018-04-16T06:20:45.230Z Reads: 92

```
What I learned from my weightweenie cycling days is that in general drilling is not a good way to reduce weight.
Even if it does not lead to failure there are many compromises especially regarding stiffness.
However I took a look at the trampa trucks and it seems you could drill a hole in the center of the baseplate without any ill effects.
I honestly do not think it is worth it as the weight reduction will be minimal.
Since I do not have dimensions of the baseplate here is an example of how you can calculate the weight you would lose if you drilled one baseplate.
Hole diameter 30mm=3cm
Plate thickness 2mm=0.2cm
Steel density 7850kg/m3=7.85g/cm3

Volume of drilled out part= t x œÄ x (d/2)^2= 0.2 x 3.14 x 0.15^2=1.413cm3
Weight=V*density=1.413 x 7.85=11.09g

Replace the numbers with the correct ones and you will have your answer.
```
